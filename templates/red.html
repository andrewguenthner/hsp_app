How Hansen Parameters Work

In brief, Hansen Solubility Parameters are a way of categorizing the solubility behavior of all known substances in a systematic way using three "parametric" dimensions. These are not real spatial dimensions, but they are a way of imagining the properties of any substance as if the substance existed at a fixed location in space. In the case of Hansen Solubility Parameters, the space is three-dimensional, and each of three Hansen Solubility Parameters (called "delta-d", "delta-p", and "delta-h") represents both an axis (or principal direction) in the "parameter space" as well as an aspect of solubility behavior. Hansen Solubility Parameters are somewhat special in that they can be given a plausible physical interpretation, in which the squared values of the parameters correspond to the amounts of various forms of energy that hold matter together. For the more chemically inclined, "delta-d>" squared corresponds to the d>ispersive energy of attraction, "delta-p" squared to dipole-dipole (polar) energy, and "delta-h>" to electron exchange energy (often described as "h>ydrogen bonding"). The crux of Hansen Solubility Parameter theory is that molecules bound by the same levels of attractive energies are indistinguishable (i.e. since the attractive forces are the same, there is no way for the surrounding molecules to notice a difference) and therefore diffuse into one another readily, a process we observe as dissolution. What this means in terms of "parameter space" is that the farther apart two substances are, the less likely they are to make a good solute-solvent pair. Semi-empirically, the theory works better if distances along the "d" axis are multiplied by two, giving rise to a special kind of "distance" in the "parameter space" called "relative energy distance" (or "RED") for short. A "RED" of zero means two substances should be perfectly compatible, while a large "RED" indicates substances will not be miscible or soluble. The "RED" is typically normalized so that values of "RED" greater than one indicate poor solubility, however, for many substances, the cut-off may not be known. In that case, "RED" values can have units (such as Joules per cubic centimeter, or J/cc, which is the most common.)
